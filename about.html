About Freelancer Tax Estimator

Last updated: May 2026

FreelancerTaxEstimator.com is a free, independent tool that helps freelancers, contractors, and sole traders get a quick, plain-English sense of their tax bill and true take-home profit. We built it because the gap between an invoice you send and the money you actually keep is one of the most confusing and stressful parts of working for yourself — especially across the different rules of the US, UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, and New Zealand.

Why we built this

Most online tax calculators are either bolted onto a sales funnel for accounting software or buried under logins and paywalls. We wanted something different: a tool you can open, use in ten seconds, and close — no signup, no email capture, and with every calculation running privately in your own browser. Alongside the calculator we publish practical, country-specific guides covering the things that actually trip freelancers up: self-employment levies, quarterly payments, registration thresholds, and the deductions people most often miss.

How we research our guides

Our country guides are written and reviewed by our editorial team using primary sources wherever possible — the official tax authorities themselves, including the IRS (US), HMRC (UK), the CRA (Canada), the ATO (Australia), the Bundeszentralamt für Steuern / Finanzamt (Germany), and Inland Revenue (New Zealand). Where rates, thresholds, or deadlines change between tax years, we update the relevant guide and refresh its "last reviewed" date.

We are not a substitute for a qualified accountant. Tax is full of edge cases — filing status, regional levies, progressive brackets, and special schemes — that no general tool can fully capture. Our goal is to help you understand the shape of your obligations and ask your accountant better questions, not to replace professional advice.

How the calculator works

The estimator applies a single, simplified flat rate per country to your taxable income (gross income minus the business expenses you enter). It then divides the result into a rough quarterly figure and shows your estimated net profit. The flat rates are deliberately conservative round numbers chosen to approximate a typical mid-income freelancer's combined income-tax-and-levies burden in each country:

  • United States — 25% (income tax plus a portion of self-employment tax)
  • United Kingdom — 20% (basic-rate income tax plus National Insurance)
  • Canada — 28% (federal and typical provincial income tax plus CPP)
  • Australia — 30% (income tax on a mid-range sole-trader income)
  • Germany — 35% (income tax plus typical social contributions)
  • New Zealand — 28% (income tax plus ACC levies)

Because real tax systems are progressive — and because everyone's deductions, thresholds, and levies differ — your actual rate may be higher or lower. Treat the output as a planning estimate, not a filing figure.
