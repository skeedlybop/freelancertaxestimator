Freelancer Taxes in Canada

Last reviewed: May 2026 · By the Freelancer Tax Estimator editorial team

Self-employed Canadians file the same T1 personal return as everyone else, but with some important twists: you pay both halves of the Canada Pension Plan, you get a later filing deadline but the same payment deadline, and once you owe enough you'll be enrolled in quarterly instalments. Understanding these quirks early prevents interest charges and a nasty surprise in your second year.

CPP contributions

As an employee, your employer matches your Canada Pension Plan contributions. As a freelancer, you pay both portions — roughly 11.9% combined on net self-employment earnings between the basic exemption ($3,500) and the annual maximum. This is on top of federal and provincial income tax, and it's the main reason self-employment can feel more heavily taxed than salaried work. Recent CPP enhancements have added a second earnings tier, so check the current year's limits.

Filing deadlines — the April 30 trap

If you or your spouse is self-employed, your return is due June 15. But here's the catch that trips up first-year freelancers: any balance owing is still due April 30. The later filing date does not extend your time to pay — interest starts accruing on unpaid tax from May 1. Many people aim to file by April 30 anyway to settle both at once.

Tax instalments

If your net tax owing is more than $3,000 ($1,800 in Quebec) in the current year and in either of the two previous years, the CRA will require quarterly instalments — due in March, June, September, and December. The CRA usually sends instalment reminders with suggested amounts; you can pay those or base instalments on your own estimate of the current year.

Key deductions

Self-employment opens up deductions employees can't claim. Common ones include:

  • A proportion of home office expenses (rent or mortgage interest, utilities, internet)
  • Business use of a vehicle, tracked with a logbook
  • Professional dues, advertising, and business insurance
  • Equipment and supplies (some via Capital Cost Allowance)

Beyond business deductions, RRSP contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar and are one of the most powerful planning tools available to Canadian freelancers.

Key dates & thresholds at a glance

  • CPP (self-employed): ~11.9% combined on eligible earnings
  • Payment due: April 30
  • Filing due (self-employed): June 15
  • Instalments triggered: net tax owing over $3,000 ($1,800 in Quebec)
  • GST/HST: register once revenue exceeds $30,000 in a 12-month period

Common mistakes freelancers make

  • Assuming the June 15 filing date means you can pay later — interest starts May 1.
  • Forgetting that CPP doubles for the self-employed.
  • Missing the $30,000 GST/HST registration threshold.
  • Not setting aside money for the first round of instalments.
