Freelancer Taxes in the United Kingdom

Last reviewed: May 2026 · By the Freelancer Tax Estimator editorial team

Going self-employed in the UK means taking on responsibilities that an employer used to handle for you through PAYE. You'll register with HMRC, file a Self Assessment tax return once a year, pay your own National Insurance, and — from your second year of trading — possibly make advance "Payments on Account." Here's how each piece fits together so you can budget properly and avoid the classic second-year cash-flow shock.

Self Assessment

Once you're self-employed, you must register for Self Assessment with HMRC. The UK tax year runs from 6 April to 5 April. You then file your return online by 31 January following the end of the tax year, and any tax owed is due by the same date. Register early — there's a deadline of 5 October after the tax year in which you started, and leaving it late risks penalties.

National Insurance contributions

Self-employed people pay National Insurance in two classes:

  • Class 2 NI — a flat weekly amount once your profits exceed the Small Profits Threshold. (Recent reforms have changed how Class 2 is treated, so check the current year's rules.)
  • Class 4 NI — charged as a percentage of profits within a band (around 9% on profits between roughly £12,570 and £50,270), then a lower rate (around 2%) above that.

NI is collected through your Self Assessment alongside income tax, so it's part of the same January bill.

Payments on Account

This is the one that catches people out. If your tax bill is above a threshold, HMRC asks you to pre-pay towards next year's tax in two instalments — 50% by 31 January and 50% by 31 July. In your second year of trading you can therefore face your first full bill plus the first payment on account at once — effectively 150% of a year's tax in one January. Budget for it.

Allowable expenses

You can deduct costs incurred "wholly and exclusively" for your business, reducing your taxable profit:

  • Equipment, software, and tools of the trade
  • A reasonable proportion of home broadband, phone, and household running costs if you work from home
  • Business travel and accommodation
  • Professional training, subscriptions, and insurance

If your income is low, the £1,000 trading allowance can be simpler than itemising — you deduct a flat £1,000 instead of actual expenses.

Key dates & thresholds at a glance

  • Tax year: 6 April – 5 April
  • Online return & payment: due 31 January
  • Payments on Account: 31 January and 31 July
  • Trading allowance: £1,000

Common mistakes freelancers make

  • Not budgeting for the second-year double payment caused by Payments on Account.
  • Registering for Self Assessment too late and triggering penalties.
  • Forgetting that NI sits on top of income tax in the same bill.
  • Keeping poor records, making the January filing a scramble.
