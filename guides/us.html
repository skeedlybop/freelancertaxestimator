Freelancer Taxes in the United States

Last reviewed: May 2026 · By the Freelancer Tax Estimator editorial team

If you freelance, contract, or run a one-person business in the US, your taxes look very different from a salaried employee's. There's no employer withholding money from each paycheck, you owe an extra layer of tax that employees never see directly, and the IRS expects you to pay throughout the year rather than in one lump sum. This guide walks through the four things that matter most — self-employment tax, quarterly payments, deductions, and whether an S-Corp is worth it — so you can plan with fewer surprises.

Self-employment tax

US freelancers pay 15.3% self-employment (SE) tax on net earnings, on top of regular income tax. This covers Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%) — both the employee and employer halves, because you are both. The Social Security portion applies up to an annual wage-base cap; Medicare has no cap and adds an extra 0.9% on high earners. The one piece of good news: you can deduct half of your SE tax when calculating your income tax, which slightly lowers your taxable income.

This is the single biggest shock for new freelancers. A contractor billing the same dollar amount a salaried colleague earns will owe noticeably more tax, because the employer share of payroll taxes that used to be invisible now lands on you.

Quarterly estimated taxes

The IRS operates on a pay-as-you-go system. If you expect to owe $1,000 or more for the year, you're generally required to make quarterly estimated payments rather than settling up only at filing time. Due dates fall roughly in mid-April, mid-June, mid-September, and mid-January of the following year. Miss them — even if you pay your full balance by the April deadline — and you can be charged an underpayment penalty.

A common safe-harbor rule: pay at least 100% of last year's tax liability (110% if your income was high) and you generally avoid penalties even if you under-estimate the current year.

Key deductions

Deductions reduce the income you're taxed on, so tracking them carefully is one of the highest-return habits a freelancer can build. Commonly claimed deductions include:

  • Home office — the simplified method allows $5 per square foot up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max), or you can use the actual-expense method.
  • Health insurance premiums — self-employed health insurance is often deductible.
  • Retirement contributions — a SEP-IRA lets you contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, sheltering a large amount from tax.
  • Equipment & software — computers, cameras, and subscriptions used for work.
  • Professional development, business travel, and a portion of phone/internet.

The S-Corp question

Freelancers earning above roughly $60,000 in profit sometimes elect to be taxed as an S-Corporation. The strategy: pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax) and take the remaining profit as distributions (not subject to SE tax), reducing your overall self-employment tax. It comes with added cost and paperwork — payroll filings, an accountant, and stricter IRS scrutiny of what counts as "reasonable" — so it's a decision to make with a CPA, not from a blog post.

Key dates & thresholds at a glance

  • SE tax rate: 15.3% on net earnings
  • Quarterly payments: required if you'll owe $1,000+; due ~April, June, September, January
  • Annual return: Form 1040 with Schedule C and Schedule SE, due mid-April
  • 1099-NEC: clients who pay you $600+ generally report it to the IRS

Common mistakes freelancers make

  • Forgetting SE tax exists and under-saving — set aside 25–30% of profit as a starting rule of thumb.
  • Skipping quarterly payments and getting hit with penalties.
  • Mixing business and personal spending, which makes deductions hard to defend.
  • Not keeping receipts and mileage logs throughout the year.
