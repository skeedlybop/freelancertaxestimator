Freelancer Taxes in Australia

Last reviewed: May 2026 · By the Freelancer Tax Estimator editorial team

Most Australian freelancers operate as sole traders, reporting business income on their personal tax return using their individual Tax File Number and an ABN. The big things to get right are knowing when you must register for GST, understanding how the ATO collects tax in advance through PAYG instalments, and making the most of deductions and super. Here's how it works.

GST registration

You must register for the Goods and Services Tax once your annual turnover reaches $75,000 AUD (you can register voluntarily below that). Once registered, you add 10% GST to your invoices, claim GST credits on business purchases, and lodge a Business Activity Statement (BAS) — usually quarterly — to the ATO. Many freelancers keep the GST they collect in a separate account so it's ready at BAS time.

PAYG instalments

Employees have tax withheld from each payslip; sole traders don't. So once your income passes a threshold, the ATO enrols you in Pay As You Go (PAYG) instalments — quarterly pre-payments towards your annual income tax. These are credited against your final bill at tax time and exist to stop you facing one enormous lump sum at the end of the financial year (which runs 1 July – 30 June).

Key deductions

You can claim expenses directly related to earning your income, including:

  • Equipment and tools — eligible assets may qualify for an instant asset write-off
  • Home office running costs, e.g. the ATO's fixed-rate method (around 67 cents per hour worked from home)
  • Vehicle expenses, supported by a logbook
  • Software, professional subscriptions, and insurance

Superannuation

Unlike employees, self-employed Australians generally aren't required to pay super — but it's worth considering anyway. Voluntary concessional (before-tax) contributions, up to the annual cap, are tax-deductible and build retirement savings that would otherwise be neglected. For many freelancers it's both a tax play and a long-term safety net.

Key dates & thresholds at a glance

  • Financial year: 1 July – 30 June
  • GST registration: required at $75,000 turnover; 10% rate
  • BAS: usually lodged quarterly
  • PAYG instalments: quarterly once enrolled
  • Tax return: due 31 October if self-lodging (later via a registered tax agent)

Common mistakes freelancers make

  • Spending the GST they collect instead of setting it aside for BAS.
  • Not registering for GST after crossing $75,000.
  • Underestimating the first PAYG instalment cycle.
  • Ignoring super entirely and missing the deduction.
