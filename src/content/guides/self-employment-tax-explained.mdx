# Self-Employment Tax Explained ({TAX_YEAR})

**Self-employment (SE) tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax that self-employed people pay on their net profit.** It is *separate from and on top of* federal income tax. For {TAX_YEAR} the combined rate is **{(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%** — **{(SE_TAX.socialSecurityRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%** for Social Security (applied to net earnings up to a wage base of **${SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase.toLocaleString()}**) and **{(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%** for Medicare (no cap, applied to all of it). It's the single biggest reason 1099 income takes home less than the same gross at a W-2 job.

<FicaPie />

## Why does this tax exist?

Every W-2 employee pays Social Security and Medicare (together called **FICA**), but they only see half of it on their pay stub. The other half is paid by the employer — out of the same compensation budget, just on a different line of the company's books. Combined, the two halves are exactly {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%.

When you're self-employed, there is no employer to pay the other half. You are the worker and the employer. The IRS doesn't let you off the hook — they collect both halves from you, and they call the combined thing **self-employment tax**.

So SE tax isn't a *penalty* on self-employed people. It's the same FICA every working American owes — you're just paying both sides of it yourself, which is why it feels so much heavier than it did when you were on W-2.

## The two halves of SE tax in {TAX_YEAR}

| Component | Rate | Applied to |
|---|---|---|
| Social Security | {(SE_TAX.socialSecurityRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% | Net earnings, **up to a wage base of ${SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase.toLocaleString()}** |
| Medicare | {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% | Net earnings, **uncapped** |
| **Combined SE tax** | **{(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%** | Up to the wage base; only the Medicare {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% applies above it |

A few things to internalize:

- The **{(SE_TAX.socialSecurityRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% Social Security portion has a ceiling.** Once your net earnings cross the wage base (${SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase.toLocaleString()} for {TAX_YEAR}), you stop paying it. High earners' SE-tax marginal rate effectively drops from {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% to {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% past that threshold.
- The **{(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% Medicare portion has no ceiling.** Every dollar of net earnings pays Medicare, all the way up.
- High earners also owe an extra **{(ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.rate * 100).toFixed(1)}% Additional Medicare tax** above filing-status thresholds (more on that below).

## The 92.35% rule (and why your "net earnings" aren't the same as your net profit)

This is the subtle step most freelancers miss when they try to calculate SE tax on the back of an envelope.

SE tax does **not** apply to your full net profit. It applies to **{(SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * 100).toFixed(2)}%** of it. The number $0.{(SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * 10000).toFixed(0)}× is sometimes called "net earnings from self-employment."

Why {(SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * 100).toFixed(2)}%? It's the IRS's algebraic way of correcting for the fact that an employee's half of FICA isn't included in their wages on the employer side. To put a self-employed person on equal footing with a W-2 employee earning the same gross, the IRS deducts {((1 - SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier) * 100).toFixed(2)}% of net profit before applying the {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% rate. The deduction approximates the "employer's half" your business would have paid in pre-FICA dollars if it were a different entity from you.

You don't need to understand the math to use it. You just need to remember:

> **SE tax = ({(SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * 100).toFixed(2)}% × net profit) × {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%** — up to the Social Security wage base, with the {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% Medicare portion continuing above it.

There is also a **floor:** if your net earnings from self-employment are below **${SE_TAX.netEarningsFloor}**, you owe no SE tax at all. That threshold has been ${SE_TAX.netEarningsFloor} since 1990; it does not adjust for inflation.

## A worked example

Let's say your {TAX_YEAR} numbers look like this:

- Gross self-employment income: **$80,000**
- Business expenses: **$15,000**
- Net profit: **$65,000**

Step 1 — apply the {(SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * 100).toFixed(2)}% rule:

> Net earnings from SE = $65,000 × {SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier.toFixed(4)} = **${netEarningsFromSE(65000).toLocaleString("en-US", { maximumFractionDigits: 0 })}**

Step 2 — apply the rate. Because ${netEarningsFromSE(65000).toLocaleString("en-US", { maximumFractionDigits: 0 })} is well under the **${SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase.toLocaleString()}** wage base, the full {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% combined rate applies:

> SE tax = ${netEarningsFromSE(65000).toLocaleString("en-US", { maximumFractionDigits: 0 })} × {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% ≈ **${Math.round(calcSelfEmploymentTax(65000)).toLocaleString()}**

That's your SE-tax line on Schedule SE. It's separate from any federal income tax you'll also owe on the same profit.

What about somebody with much higher net profit? Say net profit of $250,000:

- Net earnings = $250,000 × {SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier.toFixed(4)} = **${Math.round(250000 * SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier).toLocaleString()}**
- Social Security portion: capped at the wage base. {(SE_TAX.socialSecurityRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% × ${SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase.toLocaleString()} = **${Math.round(SE_TAX.socialSecurityRate * SE_TAX.socialSecurityWageBase).toLocaleString()}**
- Medicare portion: {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% × ${Math.round(250000 * SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier).toLocaleString()} ≈ **${Math.round(250000 * SE_TAX.netEarningsMultiplier * SE_TAX.medicareRate).toLocaleString()}**
- Total SE tax: about **${Math.round(calcSelfEmploymentTax(250000)).toLocaleString()}**

Notice that the marginal *SE-tax* rate on the dollar past the wage base is just {(SE_TAX.medicareRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%, not {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}%. This is why "1099 tax rate" varies so much across income ranges — the SE portion gets cheaper at the top, even as income tax brackets get more expensive.

## The half-SE-tax deduction

Here's a small piece of good news. Because the *employer's* half of FICA would have been a business expense if you had an employer, the IRS lets you deduct **half** of your SE tax against your *income* tax (not against SE tax itself).

It works like this:

1. You compute SE tax in full and pay it.
2. You also reduce your **Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)** by half of that SE tax. This is an "above-the-line" deduction — it doesn't depend on itemizing.
3. Your *income tax* is then calculated on the smaller AGI.

For the $65,000-net-profit example above, SE tax was about ${Math.round(calcSelfEmploymentTax(65000)).toLocaleString()}, so your AGI gets reduced by about **${Math.round(calcSelfEmploymentTax(65000) * SE_TAX.deductiblePortion).toLocaleString()}** before income tax is computed. At a 12% federal bracket, that saves you another ~${Math.round(calcSelfEmploymentTax(65000) * SE_TAX.deductiblePortion * 0.12).toLocaleString()} in income tax. Not a fortune — but it's automatic, and the calculator on the home page applies it for you.

The deduction does not reduce your SE tax itself. The IRS still wants the full {(SE_TAX.totalRate * 100).toFixed(1)}% on your net earnings.

## Additional Medicare Tax (for higher earners)

On top of regular SE tax, an **{(ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.rate * 100).toFixed(1)}% Additional Medicare Tax** applies to wages + SE earnings above a threshold that depends on filing status:

| Filing status | Additional Medicare kicks in above |
|---|---|
| Single | ${ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.thresholds.single.toLocaleString()} |
| Married filing jointly | ${ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.thresholds.marriedFilingJointly.toLocaleString()} |
| Married filing separately | ${ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.thresholds.marriedFilingSeparately.toLocaleString()} |
| Head of household | ${ADDITIONAL_MEDICARE.thresholds.headOfHousehold.toLocaleString()} |

A few things to know:

- It applies to *combined* wages + SE earnings, not each separately. A freelancer with $150,000 of net earnings plus $80,000 of W-2 wages crosses the single threshold at $200,000.
- It is **not** offset by the half-SE-tax deduction.
- For self-employed people it's paid as part of your regular tax bill; for employees it's typically withheld by the employer on wages above the threshold.

If you're nowhere near these numbers, you can safely ignore this section. If you're approaching them, the [calculator](/) on the home page accounts for it automatically.

## Common misunderstandings

- **"SE tax is on top of the highest income tax rate."** No. SE tax is on top of *income* tax, but they're computed separately. SE tax goes on Schedule SE; income tax goes on Form 1040.
- **"I can deduct SE tax to reduce SE tax."** No. You can deduct *half* of SE tax against *income* tax. SE tax itself is owed in full.
- **"I'm under $400 so I owe nothing."** You owe no *SE tax* under that floor. You can still owe *income tax* on the same income.
- **"My LLC means I don't pay SE tax."** A single-member LLC is disregarded for federal tax — you still pay SE tax on its profit. Only an S-corp election separates "reasonable salary" (payroll taxes) from "distributions" (no SE tax). See the eventual LLC vs. sole-prop guide for details.
