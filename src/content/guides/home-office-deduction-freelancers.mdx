# Home Office Deduction for Freelancers ({TAX_YEAR})

**If you use part of your home regularly and exclusively for your business, you can deduct it — and as a self-employed person, that write-off cuts both your income tax and your self-employment tax.** There are two ways to claim it: a dead-simple flat-rate method and a more involved actual-expense method. This guide covers who qualifies, how to choose, and a worked example of each.

## The two tests you must pass

The deduction hinges on two words the IRS takes literally:

- **Regular use** — you use the space for business on a continuing basis, not occasionally.
- **Exclusive use** — that space is used *only* for business. A spare room set up as your office qualifies; the kitchen table where you also eat dinner does not.

The space must also be your **principal place of business** — where you do your main work, or your admin and management. For most freelancers working from home, that's easily met. (A narrow exception drops the exclusive-use rule for licensed daycare providers.)

## Method 1 — Simplified (flat rate)

The simplest option: deduct **\${SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.ratePerSqFt} per square foot** of qualifying space, up to **{SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.maxSqFt} square feet** — a maximum deduction of **\${SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.maxDeduction.toLocaleString()}**.

> **Example:** a 150 sq ft home office → 150 × \${SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.ratePerSqFt} = **\${(150 * SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.ratePerSqFt).toLocaleString()}** deduction. No receipts, no utility math.

Use this when your space is small, your home costs are modest, or you just want zero paperwork. The trade-off is the {SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.maxSqFt}-square-foot cap and the flat rate, which often produce a smaller deduction than the actual method for renters in expensive areas.

## Method 2 — Actual expenses

Here you deduct the **business-use percentage** of your real home costs. Calculate the percentage by dividing your office's square footage by your home's total square footage, then apply it to:

- Rent or mortgage interest
- Utilities (electric, gas, water)
- Homeowners or renters insurance
- Repairs and maintenance that benefit the whole home

> **Example:** a 200 sq ft office in a 1,000 sq ft apartment = **20%** business use. If rent + utilities + insurance run \$30,000/year, your deduction is 20% × \$30,000 = **\$6,000** — well above the simplified cap.

The actual method usually wins for renters with significant housing costs, but it requires keeping records and a bit more math. You can also switch methods year to year, so it's worth running both.

## Which method should you use?

Run the quick comparison: **(your square footage × \${SIMPLIFIED_HOME_OFFICE.ratePerSqFt})** versus **(business-use % × your annual home costs)**. Take the larger one — unless the difference is small and you'd rather skip the recordkeeping the actual method demands. Either way, the deduction reduces your net profit, so it lowers SE tax too. ([See how SE tax is calculated.](/self-employment-tax-explained))

## Keep it audit-proof

The home office is one of the more scrutinized deductions, so protect it:

- **Honor "exclusive use."** Don't claim a room that doubles as a guest bedroom or playroom.
- **Photograph the space** once it's set up, so you can show it was a dedicated office.
- **Keep the supporting bills** if you use the actual method — leases, utility statements, insurance.

It's a legitimate, common deduction — claim it confidently, just keep it honest. ([Full list of freelancer deductions.](/freelancer-tax-deductions-list))
