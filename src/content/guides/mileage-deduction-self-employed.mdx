# Self-Employed Mileage Deduction ({TAX_YEAR})

**If you drive for your business, you can deduct it — and for {TAX_YEAR} the standard mileage rate is {(MILEAGE_RATE_BUSINESS * 100).toFixed(1)}¢ per business mile.** You choose between two methods (standard rate or actual costs), and either way you need a mileage log to back it up. Because the deduction reduces your net profit, it lowers both your income tax and your self-employment tax.

## The {TAX_YEAR} standard mileage rate

The IRS sets a per-mile rate each year that bundles together gas, insurance, maintenance, depreciation, and wear. For {TAX_YEAR} it's **{(MILEAGE_RATE_BUSINESS * 100).toFixed(1)}¢ per mile** driven for business.

> **Example:** 5,000 business miles × {(MILEAGE_RATE_BUSINESS * 100).toFixed(1)}¢ = **${mileageDeduction(5000).toLocaleString()}** deduction. You don't add up gas receipts — the rate covers all of it.

## Standard rate vs actual expenses

You have two ways to deduct vehicle costs, and you pick one:

- **Standard mileage method.** Multiply business miles by the {TAX_YEAR} rate. Simple, requires only a mileage log, and is usually the better deal for fuel-efficient or paid-off cars.
- **Actual expense method.** Add up all vehicle costs for the year — gas, insurance, repairs, registration, lease or depreciation — then deduct the **business-use percentage**. Better for expensive vehicles, heavy repair years, or low-MPG trucks.

A planning tip: if you want the option to use the standard rate over the life of a car, **choose it in the first year** you use the vehicle for business. You generally can't switch from actual to standard later on a vehicle where you've claimed depreciation.

## Which miles actually count

This is where freelancers most often get it wrong. Deductible business miles include:

- Driving to a client site, meeting, or job
- Trips to buy supplies or equipment for the business
- Driving between two work locations
- Trips to the bank, post office, or accountant for business

**Commuting does not count.** Driving from home to a regular workplace is personal, even if you're self-employed. One useful consequence: if your home is your principal place of business (see the [home office deduction](/home-office-deduction-freelancers)), trips from your home office to clients are business miles from the start — there's no nondeductible commute.

## The mileage log the IRS expects

A deduction you can't substantiate can be disallowed, and vehicle deductions are a common audit target. For each business trip, record:

- **Date**
- **Destination / purpose** (e.g., "client meeting — Acme Co.")
- **Miles driven**

Log it **contemporaneously** — at the time of the trip, not reconstructed from memory in April. A phone app that tracks drives automatically is the easiest way; a notebook in the glovebox works too. Also note your odometer reading at the start and end of the year.

## How much it's really worth

Because mileage reduces net profit, it saves you more than income tax alone. On that ${mileageDeduction(5000).toLocaleString()} example deduction, a freelancer also avoids self-employment tax on the same amount — so the real saving is your income-tax bracket *plus* roughly 14% in SE tax. ([How SE tax works.](/self-employment-tax-explained)) See the [full deductions list](/freelancer-tax-deductions-list) for the other write-offs that stack with this one.
