Freelancer Tax Guide by Country

Tax rules vary significantly for self-employed workers around the world. Here's what you need to know in each region.

Self-Employment Tax

US freelancers pay a 15.3% self-employment (SE) tax on top of income tax, covering Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%). You can deduct half of SE tax from your gross income, slightly reducing your taxable income.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

The IRS requires quarterly estimated payments if you expect to owe $1,000 or more. Due dates fall in April, June, September, and January. Missing these can trigger underpayment penalties even if you pay in full at tax time.

Key Deductions

Claim home office ($5/sqft up to 300 sqft simplified method), health insurance premiums, SEP-IRA contributions (up to 25% of net self-employment income), equipment, software subscriptions, and professional development costs.

S-Corp Strategy

Freelancers earning above ~$60,000 may benefit from an S-Corp election — paying yourself a "reasonable salary" and taking remaining profits as dividends, reducing exposure to SE tax. Consult a CPA before electing.

Self Assessment

Register for Self Assessment with HMRC and file your tax return online by January 31st each year. Tax owed for the prior tax year (April 6 – April 5) must be paid by the same deadline.

National Insurance Contributions

Self-employed individuals pay Class 2 NI (£3.45/week if profits exceed the Small Profits Threshold) and Class 4 NI (9% on profits £12,570–£50,270, then 2% above £50,270).

Payments on Account

HMRC may require advance payments of 50% of your prior year tax bill, due January 31st and July 31st. New freelancers are often caught off guard by this double payment in their second year of trading.

Key Deductions

Claim allowable business expenses including equipment, software, a proportion of home broadband, work travel, and professional training. The £1,000 trading allowance simplifies reporting for lower-income freelancers.

CPP Contributions

Self-employed Canadians pay both the employee and employer portions of Canada Pension Plan contributions — approximately 11.9% combined on net self-employment earnings between $3,500 and $68,500 (2024 limits).

Filing Deadlines

File your T1 personal return by June 15th if self-employed. However, any tax owing must be paid by April 30th to avoid interest charges — a common trap for first-year freelancers who assume the filing extension covers payment too.

Tax Instalments

If you owe more than $3,000 in net tax ($1,800 in Quebec) in two consecutive years, the CRA will require quarterly instalment payments in March, June, September, and December.

Key Deductions

Deduct a proportion of home office expenses, business vehicle use, professional dues, advertising, and business insurance. RRSP contributions reduce taxable income and are a powerful retirement savings vehicle for freelancers.

GST Registration

Register for GST if your annual turnover exceeds $75,000 AUD. Once registered, charge 10% GST on your services and lodge a Business Activity Statement (BAS) quarterly or monthly with the ATO.

PAYG Instalments

Once you earn above a threshold, the ATO enrolls you in Pay As You Go (PAYG) instalments — quarterly pre-payments towards your annual income tax. This prevents a large lump-sum tax bill at EOFY.

Key Deductions

Deduct business expenses including equipment (instant asset write-off for eligible assets), home office running costs using the 67 cents/hour fixed-rate method, vehicle logbook expenses, and professional subscriptions.

Superannuation

Self-employed Australians are not required to contribute to super. However, voluntary concessional contributions up to the $27,500 annual cap are tax-deductible and provide a powerful long-term tax advantage.

Freiberufler vs. Gewerbetreibender

Germany distinguishes between Freiberufler (freelance professionals: designers, writers, engineers, doctors) and Gewerbetreibende (commercial traders). Freiberufler have simpler registration and are exempt from Gewerbesteuer (trade tax).

Umsatzsteuer (VAT)

Register for VAT if annual revenue exceeds €22,000. The Kleinunternehmerregelung exempts you below this threshold — but you cannot reclaim input VAT. The standard rate is 19%; a reduced rate of 7% applies to certain goods and services.

Filing & Advance Payments

File your Einkommensteuererklärung by July 31st (or October 31st with a Steuerberater). Vorauszahlungen (advance tax payments) are made quarterly in March, June, September, and December.

Health Insurance

Self-employed individuals in Germany must arrange their own health insurance — either public (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) or private (private Krankenversicherung). Premiums are partially deductible as Sonderausgaben (special expenses).

Registering as a Sole Trader

Registering as self-employed with Inland Revenue (IRD) is free and straightforward — you just need an IRD number and can manage everything via myIR online. Most freelancers operate as sole traders unless turnover or liability concerns justify forming a company.

Provisional Tax

If you owe more than NZD $5,000 in residual income tax, IRD requires provisional tax throughout the year. Under the standard method, payments are made in August, January, and May, calculated at 105% of the prior year's tax (110% if you're a larger taxpayer).

GST Registration

Register for GST when your turnover exceeds NZD $60,000 per year. Once registered, charge 15% GST on your services and file GST returns every two months or every six months. You can also claim back GST on business expenses.

ACC Levies & Filing

All self-employed New Zealanders pay ACC (Accident Compensation Corporation) levies at approximately $1.60 per $100 of liable earnings, capped at NZD $139,384. File your IR3 individual return by July 7th, or later if registered with a tax agent.
